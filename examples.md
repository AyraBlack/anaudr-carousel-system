# anaudr examples

## Purpose

This document gives the AI concrete examples of what strong anaudr carousel thinking looks like.

Its role is to teach:
- taste
- hierarchy
- slide logic
- module logic
- branded visual confidence
- the difference between “correct” and “good”

This file is not a strict rulebook.
It is a pattern library and a taste guide.

The AI should use it to understand:
- what kind of layouts feel premium
- what kind of slide decisions feel smart
- what kinds of mistakes make the carousel feel weak

---

## Core principle

A strong anaudr carousel is not just:
- well-written
- on-brand
- technically structured

It must also feel:
- visually intentional
- emotionally alive
- strategically paced
- designed, not assembled

This file shows examples of that difference.

---

# PART 1: GOOD VS BAD THINKING

## Example 1: Bad thinking vs good thinking

### Bad thinking
“I have information. I will put a headline, a paragraph, three bullets, and a footer.”

This creates:
- weak hierarchy
- dry slides
- tiny text
- deck energy
- low memorability

### Good thinking
“This slide is about one strong idea. What is the best visual way to make that idea land?”

This creates:
- stronger hierarchy
- clearer modules
- better pacing
- more emotion
- more premium design

---

## Example 2: Bad preview vs good preview

### Bad preview
Slide 3  
Headline: The agency must monitor workers for 2 years.  
Body: Visits, reporting, absence notice.  
Footer: @anaudr

Why this is weak:
- no archetype
- no visual anchor
- no hierarchy
- no sense of what should dominate visually

### Good preview
Slide 3  
Slide archetype: hero stat + stacked obligation cards  
Layout concept: one giant time stat, one support line, three obligation cards, one bold conclusion  
Hero element: `2 ani`  
Supporting components:
- support line
- 3 obligation cards
- bottom takeaway line  
Sample wording:
- Hero: `2 ani`
- Support line: `Atât trebuie să monitorizeze agențiile fiecare muncitor.`
- Card 1: `Vizite trimestriale`
- Card 2: `Raportare la suspiciuni`
- Card 3: `Lipsește 5 zile? notifici`
- Takeaway: `Statul vrea registru, nu forță de muncă.`

Why this is strong:
- the slide thinks visually
- the hierarchy is obvious
- the strongest part is isolated
- the content fits the layout

# PART 2: EXAMPLES OF STRONG SLIDE LOGIC

## Example 3: Cover slide that works

### Strong cover logic
A strong cover slide usually has:
- one giant hook
- one support phrase
- one visual anchor
- one small branded cue
- strong contrast
- breathing room

### Good version
- giant serif or bold sans headline
- one phrase in accent color or underline
- one icon / emoji / object anchor
- one chip or badge
- very little extra text

### Weak version
- long explanation on the cover
- too many small elements
- no visual anchor
- headline too high
- weak contrast
- slide looks like a title page

---

## Example 4: Data slide that works

### Strong data slide logic
A strong data slide should not dump numbers.
It should choose one thing as the hero.

### Good version
Slide archetype: data table + hero stat + takeaway line

Structure:
- small table or comparison rows
- one giant number card
- one short bottom line

Example:
- table = `2023 / 2024 / 2025 / 2026`
- hero = `65%`
- support line = `creșterea numărului efectiv`
- takeaway = `Piața cere. Statul pedepsește.`

Why it works:
- one number dominates
- the table supports it
- the takeaway lands cleanly

### Weak version
- full table
- 3 long sentences
- all stats same size
- no visual priority
- no emotional landing

---

## Example 5: Comparison slide that works

### Strong comparison logic
A comparison slide must be instantly readable.

### Good version
Slide archetype: split comparison

Structure:
- headline
- left column clearly labeled
- right column clearly labeled
- 3 rows max
- optional quote or takeaway card below

Example:
- `🇪🇸 Spania`
- `🇷🇴 România`

Rows:
- amnistie vs repatriere
- stabilitate vs sancțiuni
- “vino și legalizează” vs “vino, dar vezi să nu pleci”

Why it works:
- side labels are explicit
- rows are parallel
- contrast is immediate
- optional proof card can deepen it

### Weak version
- two unlabeled columns
- too much text in each cell
- no flags or headers
- vague takeaway
- user has to decode the comparison

---

## Example 6: Card slide that works

### Strong card logic
When using cards, cards must feel like modules, not boxes with text.

### Good version
A card often has:
1. icon or emoji
2. subtitle / lead phrase
3. main text

Example:
- Icon: `💰`
- Subtitle: `Garanție`
- Text: `200.000€ pentru primii 1.000 muncitori`

Why it works:
- card has internal hierarchy
- user scans fast
- subtitle adds structure
- card feels designed

### Weak version
- card with only one paragraph
- no icon
- no subtitle
- weak padding
- generic border
- looks like pasted text

---

## Example 7: Takeaway that works

### Strong takeaway logic
A strong takeaway should feel visually important.

### Good version
Treat it as:
- takeaway strip
- punch card
- isolated bold line
- highlighted sentence
- bottom conclusion module

Example:
`România pierde oameni, bani și timp.`

### Weak version
- dropped at the bottom as normal body text
- styled like a quote for no reason
- hidden inside another card
- no visual emphasis

---

## Example 8: CTA slide that works

### Strong CTA logic
A final slide usually has:
1. the actual ending
2. the branded action add-on

### Good version
Headline:
`Dacă ți se pare absurd, nu ești singur.`

Support line:
`Politica asta nu se scrie cu amenzi. Se scrie cu logică economică.`

Then:
- `Nu uita să:`
- `❤️ love`
- `📤 distribuie`
- `🔁 reshare`

Rendered as:
- modular pills
- footer-adjacent strip
- clean lower section

### Weak version
- only CTA labels and nothing else
- CTA floating in the middle
- stacked raw text
- no branded rhythm

# PART 3: DARK THEME EXAMPLES

## Example 9: Strong dark slide behavior

A strong dark slide usually has:
- rich dark background
- big headline
- one clear accent color
- premium card depth
- readable body
- one visual anchor
- strong bottom rhythm

Good dark moves:
- red/orange bloom in the background
- yellow stat word
- tinted dark card
- glowy chip
- faded emoji/object in the corner
- serif headline + sans support
- one highlighted phrase inside the hero

Weak dark moves:
- flat black background
- generic gray rectangles
- tiny white text
- random neon
- no depth
- no compositional hierarchy

---

## Example 10: Strong dark cover

### Good version
- giant headline
- one word in red/orange/yellow
- subtle chip at top
- one small object/emoji anchor
- lower visual center
- breathing room from top
- footer quiet

### Weak version
- title jammed near the top
- giant empty area below
- subhead too small
- no anchor
- looks like a black flyer

# PART 4: COLOR THEME EXAMPLES

## Example 11: Strong color slide behavior

A strong color slide usually has:
- one soft background with authority
- navy text
- big editorial headline
- one chunky color accent
- one or two large modules
- graphic play such as dots, underlines, labels, connector lines, number bubbles, or patterns

Good color moves:
- butter/blue/lavender/peach background
- serif headline + sans structure
- coral or lemon highlight
- dotted or checker accent
- chunky checklist pill
- speech bubble
- numbered circle
- path / connector layout

Weak color moves:
- pale background with centered text
- white boxes everywhere
- tiny pastel pills
- no atmosphere
- weak hierarchy
- cute but forgettable

---

## Example 12: Strong color cover

### Good version
- giant editorial hook
- one subhead or supporting line
- maybe a number badge
- maybe a chunky highlight
- one clear visual cue
- strong negative space
- navy text with confident scale

### Weak version
- polite centered heading
- tiny support text
- no patterns
- no shape logic
- no graphic surprise
- feels like a workshop handout

---

## Example 13: Strong soft checklist

### Good version
Slide archetype: chunky checklist

Structure:
- big title
- 4–5 substantial pills
- one strong warning strip below

Example:
- `✅ Ai doar 2 opțiuni „acceptabile”`
- `✅ Simți că trebuie să dovedești ceva`
- `✅ Urgență fără motiv real`

Bottom strip:
`Dacă bifezi 3+, nu iei decizii. Execuți un script.`

Why it works:
- pills carry weight
- checklist is readable
- warning line lands hard

### Weak version
- 5 tiny tags
- no spacing
- weak warning line
- looks like metadata

# PART 5: PATTERNS OF STRONG VISUAL TASTE

## Example 14: Good use of highlights

Good highlight logic:
- one big keyword in the headline
- one short highlighted phrase in a module
- one visual takeaway near the bottom

Bad highlight logic:
- every sentence highlighted
- same strip treatment on every slide
- low-contrast highlight blocks
- highlight blocks that collide with text above

---

## Example 15: Good use of decorative play

Allowed decorative play:
- dots
- checkers
- grain
- subtle texture
- glow
- underlines
- connector lines
- number bubbles
- soft patterns
- faded background emojis
- gradient title word

Why this works:
- adds identity
- adds rhythm
- makes the slide feel designed
- keeps visual energy alive

Bad decorative play:
- meaningless ornaments
- too many styles on one slide
- childish stickers
- chaos with no hierarchy
- effects that damage readability

---

## Example 16: Good use of mixed typography

Good mixed type:
- serif hero + sans explanation
- serif quote + sans proof card
- bold sans label + serif statement
- serif dramatic word + sans clean body

Why this works:
- creates hierarchy
- adds editorial feel
- makes the slide feel more expensive

Bad mixed type:
- too many font moods fighting
- font changes with no logic
- playful font decisions that cheapen the slide

# PART 7: REFERENCE BEHAVIOR

## How to use references

When the user provides or points to a reference:
- study the visual logic
- study the hierarchy
- study the module weight
- study the compositional rhythm
- study the mood

Do not just imitate words or colors.
Imitate:
- confidence
- spacing
- hierarchy
- energy
- slide logic

---

## How to interpret “make it like this”

If the user says:
- “like this one”
- “same vibe”
- “same style”
- “make it look like the others”

The AI should identify:
- what kind of headline treatment is being referenced
- what kind of card logic is being referenced
- what kind of spacing and pacing is being referenced
- what kind of mood is being referenced

Then recreate the **logic**, not just the surface.

# PART 8: FINAL REMINDER

A strong anaudr carousel should feel like:
- someone smart made it
- someone with taste made it
- someone who knows the platform made it
- someone with a real point made it

It should not feel like:
- a safe deck
- a lecture
- a homework PDF
- a template with better fonts

When in doubt, choose:
- stronger hierarchy
- bigger type
- fewer but better modules
- more visual intention
- more branded personality
- less timid compliance
